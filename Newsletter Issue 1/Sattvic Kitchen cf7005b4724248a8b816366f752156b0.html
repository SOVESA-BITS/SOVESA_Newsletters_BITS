Sattvic Kitchen

by Varun (f20190681@pilani.bits-pilani.ac.in)

Bhagavad Gītā and other Vedic texts (like Āyurveda) classify food not just based on the benefits it gives to the gross body (made up of flesh, bones, muscles, nerves, etc.) but, they also consider the effect of the food on the consciousness of the eater.

Based on this, the food is classified into three categories, which are as follows:-

  1. Sāttvika/सात्त्विक: Food in the mode of goodness
  1. Rājasika/राजसिक: Food in the mode of passion
  1. Tāmasika/तामसिक: Food in the mode of ignorance

And the description of each category is given in Bhagavad Gītā—Chapter 17 Verses 8 to 10:-

आयु:सत्त्वबलारोग्यसुखप्रीतिविवर्धना: । रस्या: स्‍निग्धा: स्थिरा हृद्या आहारा: सात्त्विकप्रिया:|| āyuḥ-sattva-balārogya- sukha-prīti-vivardhanāḥ rasyāḥ snigdhāḥ sthirā hṛdyā āhārāḥ sāttvika-priyāḥ

Foods in the mode of goodness increase the duration of life, purify one's existence and give strength, health, happiness and satisfaction. Such nourishing foods are sweet, juicy, fattening and palatable.

कट्‌वम्ललवणात्युष्णतीक्ष्णरूक्षविदाहिन: । आहारा राजसस्येष्टा दु:खशोकामयप्रदा: ॥ kaṭv-amla-lavaṇāty-uṣṇa- tīkṣṇa-rūkṣa-vidāhinaḥ āhārā rājasasyeṣṭā duḥkha-śokāmaya-pradāḥ

Foods that are too bitter, too sour, salty, pungent, dry and hot, are liked by people in the modes of passion. Such foods cause pain, distress, and disease.

यातयामं गतरसं पूति पर्युषितं च यत् । उच्छिष्टमपि चामेध्यं भोजनं तामसप्रियम् ॥ yāta-yāmaṁ gata-rasaṁ pūti paryuṣitaṁ ca yat ucchiṣṭam api cāmedhyaṁ bhojanaṁ tāmasa-priyam

Food cooked more than three hours before being eaten, tasteless, stale, putrid, decomposed and unclean, is food liked by people in ignorance.

Furthermore, one who eats sāttvika food thinks in sāttvika way. He enquires about more profound truths of life. He tends to be very philosophical and curious. One who eats rājasika food is usually found to be under the influence of two feelings, hankering and lamentation. Sometimes, he may think that he is happy, but that is just an illusion. One who eats tāmasika food is always in ignorance of reality.

So, it is clear that one should try to consume sāttvika food as much as possible and avoid tāmasika and rājasika food.

Before discussing our sāttvika recipe for the month, we must address the ethics behind preparing food. To procure ingredients, we must harm lower living beings. Even if we are only taking a leaf from a giant tree, it is still an act of injury on the living tree. Also, the ingredients may be harvested, transported or processed by several people before it reaches your kitchen. All these people and yourself are involved in this act of injury.

It is not possible to nourish ourselves without injuring any living being (as also stated in Bhāgavata Mahāpurāṇa 1st Canto, 13th Chapter, 47th verse jīvo jīvasya jīvanam: "One living being is food for another.") and thus we incur sin. Therefore Kr̥ṣṇa advises that one should not just eat sāttvika food but should also offer the food to the source of the food so that he can absolve us of all sin.

Now, Can you manufacture food? No. Can I? No. We may transform the elements found in nature (minerals from the soil, water, and sunlight, etc.) and get some crops, but we didn’t create those elements in the first place, is it not? We use seeds from other plants. Life can't be made from matter. Who created plants? It is Kr̥ṣṇa Himself, the Supreme Personality of Godhead. Thus, we should acknowledge the mercy of the Lord before eating.

So, after understanding a little bit philosophy behind sāttvika food, let's try to cook one straightforward sāttvika item and offer it to the Lord.

Malpua/Malpoori

Ingredients

NameAmount
Condensed milk¼ cup
Milk 3 cups
Wheat flour1 cup
Refined flour/All purpose flour1 cup

To make the batter

Whisk the milk and the condensed milk together

Add milk and condensed milk mixture into the all-purpose flour

To make syrup

Step1. Take 1.5 cups of sugar in a vessel. Add 2.5 cups of water to it. Boil the mix. Step2. Add ¼ cup of milk. Remove the scum. Step3. Add a pinch of saffron/kesar. Step4. When the sugar syrup coats the back of the spoon, it is the right consistency. Step5. When in the right consistency, add ½ teaspoon cardamom powder. The sugar syrup is ready.

To make the malpuas

Step1. Heat two cups of ghee. Add a ladle full of batter. Step2. Flip and fry.

Step3. Drop the hot Mapuas into the sugar syrup and leave it for 5 minutes. Step4. Remove them to a plate. And the sweet Mapuas are all yours to eat!

Sidenote

Malpua is primarily a Bengali cuisine dessert. This recipe makes soft and fluffy pancakes with crisp outer edges. Malpua with Rabdi is just another incredible combination where you top up the pancake with milky Rabdi. It is an optional addition, though. We have not included the recipe of Rabdi here.
