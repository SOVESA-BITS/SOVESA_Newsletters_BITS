Jagannātha Ratha Yātrā

by Ritik (f20190805@pilani.bits-pilani.ac.in)

In the month of July, this year, one of the most significant and celebrated Vedic festivals was the

Jagannātha Ratha Yātrā on 12 July (Monday).

So, let us turn into the pages of history and see why this day is so special and is celebrated all across India as a huge festival.

A king named Indradyumna was anxious to build a temple for Kr̥ṣṇa, Balarāma, and Subhadrā. He located a sculptor to sculpt the three Deities.

The sculptor was actually the celestial architect Viśvakarmā disguise. However, the sculptor argued that he would work only in solace for the next three weeks, and as soon as he is seen by someone, he will disappear. Out of curiosity to see the deities, the king entered the workroom at the end of the first two weeks, only to find the incomplete Deities. Viśvakarmā also had disappeared from the place.

The Rathas (Sanskrit for Chariot) are immense wheeled wooden structures built anew every year and are pulled by the devotees. The chariot for Jagannātha is approximately 45 feet high and 35 feet square and takes about two months to construct.

But why do people of all ethnicities and religious backgrounds now celebrate Ratha Yātrā, a seemingly Hindu festival?  This is a very ancient festival, and its descriptions can find many references in Vedic literature. In recent days, it was propagated to the whole world by Śrīla Prabhupāda. In 1967, the first-ever Ratha Yātrā was held by him in San Francisco, New Jagannātha Puri. From then onwards, it is celebrated in all major cities of the world, including Chicago, New York, London, Paris. As stated in the Brahmāṇḍa Purāṇa, “A person who sees the Lord’s Ratha Yātrā festival and then stands up to receive the Lord can purge all kinds of sinful results from his self.” The Bhaviṣya Purāṇa, states that: “Even if born of a low family, a person who follows the chariot when the Deities pass will surely be elevated to achieving equal affluence with Lord Viṣṇu.”
